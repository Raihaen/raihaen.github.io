I have a habit of re-visiting this post every few months as I get better at the game.

The best description of catan i've ever come across was this youtube comment: 'the more the game progresses the more your inner monologue resembles that of a death note character', and I believe it sums it all up : a game of Catan is very exciting and full of emotion : hate, love, excitement, boredom...

On the surface, Catan is about navigating incomplete information and randomness, but above all else, emotions: holding grudges would just affect you and only you; many games have been lost because one stops caring about winning versus sabotaging another player -- managing the human aspect is the most important IMO. The game's pace is very irregular, you learn to navigate turns with low / high resources, and to keep a close attention on other players as they might be one $2$ roll away from changing the whole power landscape.

Catan is different in the way you learn it, at least for me[1]. Each game is relatively long, about half an hour, so you won't really play a lot of games per session; and the starting position's impact is huge: a bad starting position means gg, that half hour would be a nightmare. And so, in your first $n$ games, the first few minutes where you pick your placements will be the moments where you apply your knowledge the most (it's however also important to keep track of how did the player's placements contribute to their position and so on). You'll eventually start making sense of how resources interact with each other, how the picking order and map affect your choices... But as i was saying, you can't really start playing the real game if you didn't yet master placement picking so please don't quit the game before that, Catan is worth this initial investment just like Breaking Bad.

- [1] Note from the future: as I re-read this, I realized how ironic this sentence is, coming from someone with an olympiad background.
